                         The Life of Alma Ashby

                         born 1951, died 2003

                         a librarian,
                         from a small town on the coast





















================================================================================
PAGE 2 / 11 — CHILDHOOD
================================================================================

You were born on a Tuesday, though no one remembered this later. Your mother
held you and said nothing for a long time. The room smelled like iodine and
cut flowers.

Your father worked at the mill. He came home smelling like sawdust and would
sit in the kitchen chair without taking off his boots. You learned early that
some people carry their tiredness like a coat they can't remove.

The house had three rooms. You shared a bed with your sister until you were
eleven. She talked in her sleep — nonsense mostly, but sometimes she would say
things that sounded like instructions. Turn left. Go back. Not that one.

You were quiet in school. Teachers wrote "pleasant" on your report cards, which
you understood even then was a word for children who didn't cause problems.
You read under your desk. You read during recess. You read while walking home
and once stepped into the street without looking and a man on a bicycle shouted
something you pretended not to hear.

Your mother kept a garden. Tomatoes, herbs, flowers whose names you memorized
and then forgot and then memorized again. She taught you that some things grow
back and some things don't and you won't always know which is which until it's
too late.

You had a dog named Captain. He was not a good dog. You loved him anyway.

Summers were long. The light lasted until nine and you would sit on the porch
and listen to the radio through the screen door. The world was enormous and
somewhere else. You were not unhappy.















================================================================================
PAGE 3 / 11 — ADOLESCENCE
================================================================================

At fourteen you kissed David Park behind the gymnasium and afterward felt
nothing in particular. You had expected something — loss, or knowledge, or at
least a sense of having crossed a line. Instead you walked home and ate an
apple and did your homework.

You discovered the town library the way some people discover religion: all at
once and completely. The librarian was a woman named Mrs. Okafor who never
smiled but who would set books aside for you without being asked. She seemed
to know what you needed before you did. You wanted to be her.

Your sister left for college. The bed felt enormous. You spread out your books
across her side and slept in a fortress of paperbacks.

Your father got sick that winter. Not seriously — a cough that wouldn't leave,
a tiredness that went deeper than the usual tiredness. He sat in his chair and
watched the snow fall and didn't say anything about the mill.

You applied to three colleges and got into two. You chose the cheaper one. Your
mother cried, not because you were leaving but because she was proud, and
pride was not an emotion she trusted.

The last night before you left, you sat on the porch and the radio played
something you didn't recognize. The stars were out. Captain was dead by then —
had been for two years — but you still looked for him in the yard.

You took a bus. You watched your town get smaller in the rear window until it
was just a smudge, and then just a direction, and then just a memory you
would carry like a stone in your pocket for the rest of your life.













================================================================================
PAGE 4 / 11 — EARLY ADULTHOOD
================================================================================

College was smaller than you expected. Your roommate was from somewhere warm
and talked about the ocean as though it were a person she missed. You studied
literature and then switched to library science because you wanted to be
useful, and literature didn't feel useful, even though it was.

You graduated on a Saturday. Your parents drove four hours. Your father wore
a tie and looked uncomfortable. Your mother took photographs with a camera she
had borrowed from a neighbor. You looked serious in all of them.

Your first job was at a branch library in a town you had never heard of. The
building was brick and the heating didn't work properly and the collection was
small and earnest. You shelved books and helped old men find newspapers and
read to children on Tuesday afternoons. The children were skeptical of you at
first but warmed up when you did the voices.

You rented a studio apartment above a hardware store. The floor sloped slightly
toward the east wall. You put your bed against the west wall and your books
against the east wall as a counterbalance. This was a joke you made to yourself
and never told anyone.

You dated a man named Paul who fixed motorcycles. He was kind and had large
hands and laughed at things that weren't funny in a way that made them funny.
It didn't work out. These things often don't. You remained friendly in the way
that means you nodded at each other in the grocery store.

You got a cat. You named her Volumes. The vet thought that was strange. You
didn't care.














================================================================================
PAGE 5 / 11 — MIDDLE YEARS (WORK)
================================================================================

You were promoted. Then promoted again. Then you were the head librarian, which
meant you ordered the books and managed the volunteers and fixed the printer
when it jammed, which was often. You were good at this. You did not think of
it as your calling because you didn't believe in callings, but if you had, this
would have been it.

The library expanded. You fought for the funding yourself — sat in meetings
with town councillors who did not read and explained, patiently, why a library
mattered. You used the word "community" thirty-seven times in one meeting. You
counted.

A child once asked you if you had read every book in the library. You said no.
She asked why not. You said there wasn't time. She looked at you with the kind
of pity only children are capable of.

You started a reading program for adults who had never learned. A man named
Gerald, sixty years old, read his first complete sentence in your back office
on a Wednesday afternoon in November. It said: "The cat sat on the mat." He
cried. You did not cry, but you went home and sat in your kitchen for a long
time without turning on the lights.

The building needed a new roof. Then new wiring. Then new plumbing. You
learned more about infrastructure than you had ever wanted to know. You
developed opinions about copper pipes.

You went to conferences. You stayed in hotels that all looked the same. You
ate continental breakfasts and attended panels about digital cataloguing and
metadata standards. You met other librarians. They were, without exception,
the most interesting people you had ever known, and no one outside the
profession would have believed you if you'd said so.











================================================================================
PAGE 6 / 11 — MIDDLE YEARS (PERSONAL)
================================================================================

Your mother died on a Thursday. You drove home and the house was exactly as
you remembered except smaller. Your sister was already there. You sat together
in the kitchen and drank tea and neither of you cried, not because you weren't
sad but because you were your mother's daughters.

You kept the garden going for a year. Then you sold the house.

Your father had died three years earlier. A stroke, quick, in the armchair.
The television was still on when they found him. You always wondered what he
had been watching.

You dated occasionally. A woman named Claire who taught high school chemistry.
A man whose name you can't quite recall — isn't that terrible — who sold
insurance and was very earnest about it. Neither lasted. You were not lonely
exactly, but you were alone, and those are different things that sometimes
feel the same.

Volumes died. You buried her in the backyard of your apartment, which was
technically not your backyard, but the landlord pretended not to notice. You
got another cat. You named her Footnote. You were aware of the pattern.

You turned forty. Then fifty. The years had a way of folding into each other
like pages in a book left out in the rain. You could not always remember which
year something had happened — was it the winter the pipes froze or the winter
you learned to make bread? — and you began to suspect that it didn't matter,
that time was less a line than a room you kept rearranging.

You took up walking. Long walks, early morning, before the town woke up. You
liked the emptiness of it. The street lamps still on. The dew. You thought
your best thoughts while walking and forgot them by the time you got home.










================================================================================
PAGE 7 / 11 — LATER YEARS
================================================================================

You retired. They gave you a plaque. You put it in a drawer.

The new librarian was young and enthusiastic and believed in social media. You
wished her well and meant it. You went back once, to return a book, and
everything was different and everything was the same.

You traveled. Not far — you had never been someone who needed to go far. The
coast. The mountains once. A city where you visited a museum and stood in front
of a painting for forty minutes and felt something you couldn't name.

Your sister called every Sunday. You talked about nothing in particular. The
weather. What you'd eaten. Whether the show you were both watching was getting
worse or if you were just getting older. These calls were the scaffolding of
your week and you would have been embarrassed to admit how much they mattered.

You read. Of course you read. You reread books you'd loved at twenty and found
them different. Not worse, not better — different. You were a different reader
now. A different person. The books hadn't changed. You had. This seemed
important but you couldn't say why.

You started writing things down. Not a diary — you had never trusted diaries —
but notes. Observations. The way the light hit the kitchen counter at 4 PM in
September. The sound of rain on the roof when the wind came from the east. You
filled three notebooks and then stopped. You didn't throw them away.

Footnote was old now too. She slept in a patch of sunlight on the couch and
purred when you sat beside her. You were both creatures of habit and you were
both content, which is not the same as happy but is, perhaps, more durable.













================================================================================
PAGE 8 / 11 — THE LAST YEAR
================================================================================

The doctor said it was nothing to worry about. Then he said it was something
to keep an eye on. Then he used words you recognized from books you had shelved
but never read.

You told your sister. She drove up the next day. She sat in your kitchen and
held your hand and you both looked out the window at the garden you had finally
started, thirty years after your mother had tried to teach you.

The treatments were not as bad as you had feared. They were worse in a
different way — not the pain but the waiting. The fluorescent lights in the
clinic. The magazines from last year. The other patients who smiled at you
with a solidarity you had not asked for but could not refuse.

You kept walking. Slower now, and not as far, but you kept walking. The morning
air was the same morning air. The street lamps still came on too early in the
evening and stayed on too late in the morning. You had opinions about this.

You read less. This was the thing that frightened you most — not the diagnosis,
not the prognosis, but the fact that you could hold a book in your hands and
not want to open it. You had not known that was possible.

Footnote died in March. You did not get another cat.

Summer came. The garden grew despite your inattention. Tomatoes, herbs, flowers
whose names you had memorized and forgotten and memorized and forgotten. Your
mother's garden, in the end. You sat on the porch and listened to nothing in
particular and the light lasted until nine.














================================================================================
PAGE 9 / 11 — THE LAST DAY
================================================================================

You were thinking about what to have for dinner.

It was a Tuesday — another Tuesday, the bookend to the one you were born on,
though you didn't know that and wouldn't have cared. The light in the kitchen
was good. Late afternoon, September. The counter was warm where the sun hit it.

You had a book open on the table. You had always had a book open on the table.
This one was about a woman who walked across Spain and you were on page 200 and
you would never know how it ended.

You stood up to check if there was soup in the pantry. There was. Tomato. You
took it down from the shelf and set it on the counter and then you were on the
floor.

It didn't hurt, which surprised you. Later you would think about this — or
something that had been you would think about this — and wonder whether the
absence of pain was a kindness or just an indifference. But that was later.

Right now: the floor was cool against your cheek. The linoleum. You could see
under the refrigerator, where there was dust and a pen cap you had lost months
ago. You thought: there it is. And then you thought nothing at all.






















================================================================================
PAGE 10 / 11 — AFTER
================================================================================

And then you were here.

You don't remember arriving. One moment: the kitchen floor, the pen cap, the
soup. The next: a corridor that stretched in both directions farther than you
could see, lined with books. Calfskin bindings, gilded edges. All identical.
All different.

A man was there. He explained, patiently, in a language you understood but had
never spoken, that you were dead. That this was not heaven, not exactly, but a
place where things were sorted out. That somewhere in this library — this
impossible, infinite library — was a book that contained your life. Every word
you had ever spoken. Every thought you had kept to yourself. Every morning you
had woken up and made coffee or hadn't.

Find it, he said. Submit it. And then you can go.

You asked: go where?

He didn't answer.
